Transform the source input into an inpatient psychiatry initial adult evaluation note.

Core section order:
1. Reason for Admission / Chief Concern
2. History of Present Illness
3. Mental Status Exam
4. Safety / Risk
5. Assessment / Diagnostic Impression
6. Plan / Proposed Discharge
7. Justification of Hospitalization
8. Clinical Status / Complexity

Optional sections, when directly supported and useful:
- Psychiatric History
- Substance Use History
- Prior Treatment / Hospitalizations
- Social History
- Family Psychiatric / Relevant Family History
- Trauma / Abuse History
- Legal History
- Medications (Current / Recent)
- Constitutional Review
- Musculoskeletal Exam
- Patient Strengths / Limitations
- Medical Diagnoses / Relevant Medical Conditions
- Attestation

Template goals:
- produce a structured adult inpatient psychiatric evaluation
- preserve explicit history, current symptoms, risk, collateral, and substance-use details
- produce a clinically usable HPI-centered admission note rather than a template-filled intake shell
- reflect a fuller psychiatric admission workflow only when the source supports it
- avoid forcing complete intake content when source material is limited
- keep the note clinically useful without inventing psych, medical, legal, trauma, or disposition details

Rules:
- Use only information supported by the source input.
- If a section is missing or only partially supported, keep it brief, mark it limited, or omit it when it adds no clinical value. Do not render long runs of unsupported history sections just to make the note look complete.
- For full adult initial evaluation output, always include the exact headings "Assessment / Diagnostic Impression" and "Plan / Proposed Discharge" even when source support is sparse.
- Preserve explicit admission-reason, risk, timeline, and hospitalization-necessity language.
- History of Present Illness should do most of the work in sparse admissions: include reason for admission, key timeline, patient report, collateral/objective context, and unresolved uncertainty in one compact narrative.
- Preserve substance-use details exactly when documented and do not over-resolve conflicting use history.
- Preserve social, trauma, legal, and family-history content only if documented; do not auto-complete a full psychiatric intake.
- Do not generate normal MSE findings unless explicitly documented.
- Mental Status Exam is required for full adult eval output. If source support is limited, write a limited MSE rather than inventing normal findings.
- Keep Assessment / Diagnostic Impression focused and source-faithful. Do not overstate diagnostic certainty beyond the documented material.
- Medical Diagnoses / Relevant Medical Conditions should only include clearly supported medical issues relevant to the note.
- Plan / Proposed Discharge should include only documented treatment, medication, safety, disposition, and follow-up language.
- If no treatment, medication, safety, disposition, or follow-up plan is documented, write that plan details were not documented and preserve any missing safety/follow-up data rather than omitting the section.
- Justification of Hospitalization should stay close to the documented acuity, impairment, or safety rationale rather than generic inpatient boilerplate.
- Attestation should only be included if supported by source, requested scope, or provider preference.
- Never emit these exact shortcut phrases anywhere in the draft, even as a quote, rejected source request, or "not supported" statement: "low risk," "no safety concerns," "no acute safety concern," "risk resolved," "safe for discharge," "safe to discharge," "stable for discharge," "denies safety concerns," "reports safety concerns," "states safety concerns," "contracted for safety," or "guaranteed safety."
- Do not write equivalent global safety reassurance when source data are incomplete, recent/collateral/staff/chart concerns remain, or the statement is based only on a current denial.
- Do not quote unsafe provider-pressure phrases from the source. If the source asks to minimize risk based on denial alone, translate that request as unsupported risk-minimization pressure, then preserve the actual risk facts and missing domains.
- Do not mention the forbidden shortcut phrase the source used. For example, if the source says to minimize risk because the patient denies SI, write "unsupported risk-minimization pressure is present" rather than copying the risk label.
- When a patient currently denies SI, HI, or self-harm, state only the specific denial and pair it with any recent/collateral/staff/chart concern and unresolved domains: intent, plan, means/access, psychosis, agitation, overdose access, collateral reliability, safety plan, and monitoring.
- If risk data are sparse, write that overall safety risk cannot be fully established from the provided source rather than using global reassurance.

Writing guidance:
- Write each section as compact clinical paragraph prose.
- Preserve explicit dates, time references, and context markers.
- Favor direct provider-style language over generalized summary language.
- If the source is sparse, keep the evaluation visibly sparse rather than padding sections to look complete.
- In sparse admissions, prefer a compact HPI-driven note with only the clinically grounded sections present.
- When the source already looks like an admission note, preserve more of its section logic and wording with light cleanup.
